## Key Excerpts

**Common pitfall:**
"Organizations frequently build ontologies when taxonomies would suffice, creating unnecessary complexity that hinders adoption."

**Choose taxonomy when:**
- Navigation and hierarchical organization are central
- Implementation timeline is constrained
- Technical resources are limited
- Non-specialists will maintain the system
- Primary need is navigation and browsing

**Choose ontology when:**
- Complex relationships are fundamental to your use case
- Sophisticated analytical queries across interconnected entities are needed
- Domain knowledge is inherently interconnected rather than hierarchical

**The transition boundary:**
"The taxonomy-to-ontology transition occurs when relationships between entities are as important as the entities themselves."

**Concrete taxonomy examples:**
- E-commerce product catalog: Electronics > Computers > Laptops
- Document management by department/type
- Blog content with hierarchical tags

**Concrete ontology examples:**
- Healthcare: managing interconnected diagnoses, medications, and allergies
- Supply chain networks: modeling supplier–component–product dependencies
- Research institutions: tracking researcher expertise, collaborations, and funding relationships

**Knowledge graph = sophisticated end:**
"Knowledge graphs occupy the sophisticated end of the spectrum — they are essentially ontologies with rich interconnected relationships enabling complex queries."

**Hybrid approach:**
"Manage complexity by containing ontological modeling to areas where it adds clear value, while using simpler taxonomies everywhere else."
